### 📌 Evidence Block 2: Algorithm Efficiency Creates Hardware Asset Appreciation

**Context**: Why are older-generation GPUs (H100) sometimes worth *more* today than at launch?

**🗣️ Original quote**:
> "The value of an H100 is now predicated on the value that GPD 5.4 can get out of it instead of the value that GP4 can get out of it... GPD 5.4 is both way cheaper to run than GP4, has fewer active parameters... So when you look at an H100, it can produce more tokens of a model that is of higher quality... So you sort of have this dynamic that is quite interesting in that an H100 is worth more today than it was 3 years ago."

**🇨🇳 Translation**:
An H100's asset value is not static — it depends on the efficiency of the model running on it. Newer sparse models (e.g., GPT-5-class) have fewer active parameters but higher output quality due to post-training techniques like reinforcement learning. As a result, each H100 now generates more high-quality tokens per dollar than it did at launch, reversing the typical hardware depreciation curve.

## Chapter 3: The 2030 Physical Ceiling — ASML EUV Capacity

### 📌 Evidence Block 3: How Many EUV Tools Does 1 GW Actually Require?

**Context**: Quantifying exactly how much of ASML's scarce EUV capacity is consumed by building 1 GW of compute infrastructure.

**🗣️ Original quote**:
> "A gigawatt of Nvidia's Ruben chips... you need about 55,000 wafers of 3 nanometer... and 170,000 wafers of DRAM. You're at roughly 2 million EUV passes for a single gigawatt. An EUV tool can do roughly 75 wafers per hour... you end up with actually I need about three and a half EUV tools to do the 2 million EUV wafer passes for the gigawatt."

**🇨🇳 Translation**:
Building 1 GW of compute using Nvidia's latest Rubin architecture requires approximately 55,000 logic wafers and 170,000 DRAM wafers. These together demand ~2 million EUV exposures. At a throughput of ~75 wafers/hour per machine, this consumes the full annual capacity of approximately 3.5 top-tier EUV tools — just for 1 GW.
